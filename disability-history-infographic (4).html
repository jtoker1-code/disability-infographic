From Institutions to Community

The History of Institutionalization and Deinstitutionalization

The Historical Issue

For over a century, people with intellectual and developmental disabilities were systematically segregated from society and confined to large state institutions. These facilities, often called "schools" or "hospitals," promised care and education but delivered overcrowding, neglect, abuse, and inhumane conditions (Minnesota Governor's Council on Developmental Disabilities, n.d.). The institutional era represents one of the darkest chapters in disability history, affecting millions of individuals and their families. The exposure of these conditions in the 1960s and 1970s sparked a nationwide movement toward deinstitutionalization that fundamentally changed how we support people with disabilities today (New York Civil Liberties Union [NYCLU], 2024).

Key Historical Moments

1800s-1900s

Rise of Institutions

Large state institutions were built across the United States, initially promising education and treatment for people with disabilities. However, these facilities quickly became overcrowded warehouses where people were segregated from society, stripped of dignity, and forgotten (Minnesota Governor's Council on Developmental Disabilities, n.d.).

1947

Willowbrook State School Opens

Willowbrook State School opened in Staten Island, New York, designed to house 4,000 residents. Within years, it became severely overcrowded with over 6,000 people with developmental disabilities living in squalid, dangerous conditions with inadequate staff and resources (New York Civil Liberties Union [NYCLU], 2024).

1972

Willowbrook Exposed

Journalist Geraldo Rivera broadcast footage from inside Willowbrook, showing people living in their own waste, naked and neglected, with rampant disease and abuse. The expos√© shocked millions of Americans and galvanized public demand for change (Minnesota Governor's Council on Developmental Disabilities, n.d.).

1975

Willowbrook Consent Decree

Parents filed a landmark class-action lawsuit resulting in the Willowbrook Consent Decree. The court ordered New York State to improve conditions and transition residents to community-based care, establishing standards for treatment and the right to live in the least restrictive environment (NYCLU, 2024).

1987

Willowbrook Closes

Willowbrook State School officially closed after a 15-year effort to transition residents to community settings. The closure symbolized the national deinstitutionalization movement and became a cornerstone of the disability rights movement (Office for People With Developmental Disabilities [OPWDD], 2025).

Why This History Matters Today

Understanding this history is essential for providing quality services. The institutional era teaches us that segregating and devaluing people with disabilities leads to abuse, neglect, and violation of basic human rights. When people are congregated and isolated from society, they become invisible and vulnerable.

This history reminds us that community inclusion is a hard-won right, not a privilege. The brave advocacy of people with disabilities, their families, journalists, and attorneys forced systemic change. The services we provide today‚Äîcommunity residences, supported employment, inclusive education‚Äîexist because people fought against institutionalization.

We must remain vigilant. While large institutions have closed, concerns about "reinstitutionalization" persist when people with disabilities are placed in large congregate settings, nursing homes, or isolated from community life. Quality services today must honor the lessons of history by prioritizing choice, dignity, community integration, and the voices of people with disabilities themselves (NYCLU, 2024).

Every person deserves to live, work, and thrive in their community. As service providers, we have a responsibility to remember this history and ensure that people with disabilities receive supports that promote independence, community participation, and full inclusion‚Äînever returning to the segregation and dehumanization of the institutional era (OPWDD, 2025).
